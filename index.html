ThirdEyeOpen

It all started as a small project—nothing big, just a simple way to share my creative work with the world. I never expected it to become something so vibrant, a space where I could showcase my comics, videos, animations, and the endless flow of ideas bouncing around in my head. Initially, it was a personal experiment, a little corner of the internet where I could store and share my creations. But soon, it took on a life of its own, evolving from a casual side project to a growing hub for stories and visual experiences that connect people to my world.

The comics were the first to take shape. They began as quick sketches, funny little strips that captured random thoughts or ridiculous scenarios. I shared them, thinking maybe a handful of people would enjoy them, but they started gaining attention. People resonated with the characters, laughed at their quirks, and waited for new updates. It was then that I realized how something as simple as a few frames could communicate emotions, humor, and sometimes even meaningful messages. The response was encouraging, and it made me want to push my boundaries, to try something more ambitious.

So I branched out to videos. I wasn’t a filmmaker by any stretch, but I was curious about how to bring stories to life through movement, dialogue, and sound. I wanted to create something more dynamic, something that would capture a different kind of attention. My first videos were raw, a bit shaky, maybe even messy, but they were real. And just like with the comics, people connected with them. Each project became an opportunity to learn something new, whether it was about editing, storytelling, or simply the art of timing a punchline. The feedback kept me motivated to dive deeper, to improve, to make each video better than the last.

And then came the animations—a whole new challenge and a game-changer in terms of creativity. Animation allowed me to blend the world of comics with the fluidity of video, creating something that felt truly alive. Bringing my characters to life through animation was like watching my imagination in real-time. I experimented with different styles, tested out some wild effects, and let my imagination roam freely. The process was tough; each animation took hours of dedication, but the outcome was always worth it. Each piece felt like a small movie, with its own tiny universe filled with characters, colors, and stories.

Today, this project has transformed into a collective journey. It’s no longer just a repository of things I make—it's become a shared experience, something people look forward to exploring, whether it's a new comic strip, a quirky video, or an animated story. Each piece I release is a new chapter, an opportunity to connect, laugh, and feel. And the best part? It's only just begun. The ideas are endless, the stories are limitless, and the world of possibilities keeps expanding.

So keep watching, keep enjoying, and let’s see where this adventure takes us. As long as there are stories to tell and ideas to share, I’ll be here, creating, crafting, and sharing it all with you.

Constantine John Phipps was the first to describe the polar bear as a distinct species in 1774 in his report about his 1773 expedition towards the North Pole. He chose the scientific name Ursus maritimus, the Latin for "maritime bear", due to the animal's native habitat. The Inuit refer to the animal as nanook (transliterated as nanuq in the Inupiat language). The Yupik also refer to the bear as nanuuk in Siberian Yupik. In the Chukotko-Kamchatkan languages of Alyutor and Koryak, the name of the polar bear is umqa, while in the related Chukchi, it is umqə. In Russian, it is usually called бе́лый медве́дь (bélyj medvédj, 'white bear'), though an older word still in use is ошку́й (Oshkúj, which comes from the Komi oski, "bear"). In Quebec, the polar bear is referred to by the french terms ours blanc ('white bear') or ours polaire ('polar bear'). In Norwegian, one of the primary languages of the Svalbard archipelago, the polar bear is referred to as isbjørn ('ice bear') or kvitbjørn ('white bear').

The polar bear was previously considered to be in its own genus, Thalarctos. However, evidence of hybrids between polar bears and brown bears, and of the recent evolutionary divergence of the two species, does not support the establishment of this separate genus, and the accepted scientific name is now therefore Ursus maritimus, as Phipps originally proposed.
